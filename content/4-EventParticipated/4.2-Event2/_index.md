# Reflection Report: “Cloud Architect Game Show”

### Event Objectives

- **Testing "battle-tested" knowledge:** Bringing dry theories about Cloud and Architecture Design into a high-pressure environment that requires players to think on their feet and be highly precise.
- **Honing Decision-Making skills:** Through the use of tactical "power-ups", interns learn how to conduct Risk Assessment in uncertain situations.
- **Networking and breaking the ice:** Creating opportunities for cross-interaction by randomly grouping interns from different teams together, practicing Agile Teamwork skills.

---

### Summary of Format & Rules

Unlike one-way seminars, this event was an extremely thrilling direct head-to-head intellectual arena:

#### 1. Scale & Head-to-Head Format
- **Lineup:** A total of 8 teams participated, each consisting of exactly 5 members (encouraging diverse team formations, not limited to the same internal project, and specifically "banning" veteran experts to ensure fairness).
- **Rounds:** Two teams faced off directly (1vs1), taking turns solving a set of questions from easy to hard. The team with the higher score secured a ticket to the next round.

#### 2. Tie-break Rule (Golden Goal)
- In the event of a tie after the official questions, the two teams entered the **sudden-death question number 11**. 
- The rule shifted to a "Fastest Finger" format – the team that locked in the correct answer faster instantly won the overall match.

#### 3. Tactical Mechanics (Power-ups)
The highlight of the Game Show lay in risk management through 2 special skills (each team could only use each once):
- **Minimum Risk (Defense):** Activated when a team faced a "tough" question and wasn't confident. If the answer was wrong, the team's score was preserved. If correct, the team only received 50% of the points. This was a trade-off for safety.
- **Star of Hope (Offense):** Activated on a "strong-suit" question. A correct answer yielded double points (x2), but a wrong one resulted in a double deduction. An "All or Nothing" move.

---

### Reflections & Practical Applications

The experience of "rapid-fire thinking" under the Game Show's time pressure brought perspectives remarkably similar to the real-world work of a Backend Developer when operating systems:

1. **Risk Management Mindset:** Using the *Star of Hope* or *Minimum Risk* is exactly like making decisions when running a real-world system. When designing the Backend architecture for the forecasting problem, if we aren't certain about the system load, we must choose the safe option (allocating resources reasonably) instead of going "all-in," which could waste Cloud costs or crash the server.
2. **Incident Response Skills:** Having 5 heads discussing at high speed to agree on an answer within dozens of seconds feels very much like the team "firefighting" (debugging/hotfixing) when the system encounters an error in the Production environment. Communication must be extremely concise, relying heavily on teammates' expertise.
3. **Filling Knowledge Gaps:** The hard to extremely hard questions on Cloud Architecture exposed theoretical gaps that I hadn't fully grasped. Thanks to this, I know exactly which AWS documentation I need to read further to directly optimize the infrastructure for the current project.
4. **The Power of a Cross-Functional Team:** Teaming up with peers from different domains helped me realize that a perfect system requires multi-dimensional perspectives (from Network, Security, to Database optimization), rather than just purely writing code logic.

> **Conclusion:** "Cloud Architect" was not just an entertaining playground after stressful coding hours, but also a practical test of foundational knowledge and psychological resilience. The event helped me bond more with fellow interns and take away a long list of Cloud concepts that need deeper exploration.
